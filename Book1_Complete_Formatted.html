THE BINDING

The Cursed Academy, Book 1

She came to prove herself. She stayed because of him.

When Lyra Ashford receives an invitation to the prestigious Veranthos Academy, it's her chance to escape her past and master the magic that's always been just out of reach.

She didn't expect Damian Vane.

Cold, arrogant, and impossibly skilled, Damian is everything Lyra hates—and everything she can't stop thinking about. Their rivalry ignites the moment they meet, and the academy walls seem to crackle with tension whenever they're near.

But something is wrong at Veranthos. Ancient magic pulses beneath the stone. Students are disappearing. And Lyra keeps hearing a voice that isn't a voice—a whisper from the curse that runs through her blood.

When a forbidden ritual goes wrong, Lyra and Damian are bound together by magic neither of them controls. Now their pain is shared, their thoughts bleed through, and the academy's darkest secret is unraveling.

To survive, Lyra must trust the one person she sworn to hate. To break the curse, she must face the truth about her past.

But some bonds are meant to break. And others are meant to last.

Chapter 1 – Arrival & First Blood

The sky over the academy was a bruised violet, the last gasp of dawn bleeding into a thin, cold mist that curled around the ancient stone walls like a living thing. From the high, arched windows of the east wing, the light fell in thin shafts, catching dust motes that floated like tiny, reluctant fireflies. The great iron gates of the Cursed Academy creaked open on their own accord, as if the building itself sensed the arrival of new blood.

Lyra Ravenshade stepped through the threshold with her boots barely making a sound on the flagstones. She was sixteen, though the weight of the world seemed to have aged her a decade. Her dark hair, streaked with a silver sheen from the fire that had claimed her village, fell in a tangled mane around a face that was both fierce and weary. A scar cut a shallow line across her left cheek—proof of the night the flames took everything but her spirit.

She glanced up at the towering spires that rose like skeletal fingers into the sky, each one capped with a glimmering rune that pulsed faintly, as if the stones themselves were still humming with ancient magic. The academy’s name—Arcane Academy of the Cursed Arts—was etched in gilt letters across the central archway, the words curling like vines around each other, promising both knowledge and danger.

A murmur rose from the courtyard as other newcomers filtered in: cloaked boys and girls, each bearing the mark of a different house, each clutching a satchel of scrolls and a nervous hope. Lyra’s eyes flicked to a tall, silver‑haired boy in a black coat, his posture immaculate, his stare cold enough to cut steel. He was the first to notice her, his violet eyes locking onto hers for a heartbeat before he turned his attention to the instructor who was setting up a dueling arena in the center of the courtyard.

“Welcome, newcomers,” intoned Master Voss, his voice echoing off the stone. “You have been chosen for your potential, your lineage, and—most importantly—your willingness to face the curse that binds us all. Here, you will learn to bend darkness to your will, to wield the forbidden arts without losing yourself. You will fight, you will fall, and you may—if you are fortunate—rise stronger.”

Lyra felt a prickle along her spine. The words “forbidden arts” were a promise of the power she’d always craved, and also a reminder of the price her family had paid. The fire that had taken her home had left her with a whisper of a curse she barely understood—an old family binding that made her blood sing when dark magic was spoken. She had come to the academy hoping to uncover its origin and, perhaps, break it.

The instructor gestured toward a pair of wooden dummies set up at opposite ends of the arena. “To begin, you will pair off and demonstrate your combat proficiency. The duel will not be to the death, but to the first touch. Show us your skill, your resolve.”

Lyra’s heart hammered. She had sparred with the village’s blacksmith’s son, learned to wield a short blade, but nothing compared to the polished steel that gleamed in the boy’s hand as he approached. The silver‑haired youth—Damian Blackthorn—stopped a few paces away, his coat shifting with each step, the faint scent of incense and iron trailing behind him.

“You’re Lyra Ravenshade,” he said, voice smooth, laced with a faint sarcasm. “I’ve heard about the fire. Tragic—though I suspect the real tragedy will be you trying to survive my training.”

Lyra raised an eyebrow, the scar on her cheek catching the morning light. “And you must be Damian Blackthorn, heir to the Vekri noble house. I’ve heard you’re as arrogant as your family’s reputation suggests.”

A thin smile curled Damian’s lips. “Arrogance is just confidence misnamed. Let’s see if your fire can match my ice.”

Without further ceremony, they took their positions. The arena was a simple rectangle of sand, the edges marked by low, rune‑etched stones that seemed to pulse with a faint amber glow. The crowd of onlookers—students, a few senior instructors, even a few older wizards perched on stone balustrades—fell into a hush as the duel began.

Lyra drew her short blade, a hand‑forged steel she’d claimed from the ruins of her home. The blade was light, its edge honed to a whisper. She planted her feet, feeling the sand shift beneath her, and took a breath that seemed to draw in the very mist that swirled around the courtyard.

Damian unsheathed a sleek rapier, the metal singing as it left its scabbard. He moved with a predatory grace, his cloak fluttering like a dark banner. In an instant, the space between them crackled with tension—two forces on the brink of collision.

The first strike was a blur. Lyra lunged forward, her blade aiming for his shoulder. Damian pivoted, parrying with his rapier, the clang of steel echoing off the stone walls. Their eyes locked—her storm‑gray gaze meeting his icy violet—each trying to read the other’s intent.

“Too slow,” Damian muttered, thrusting forward, his blade slicing a shallow cut across Lyra’s forearm. The touch was brief but enough to draw a thin line of blood, bright against her skin. The pain was sharp, but as the blade met her flesh, something else surged—an electric tingling that ran from the wound up her arm, flickering through her veins like a distant storm.

Lyra’s breath caught. The instant the blade pierced her, she felt a sudden, foreign sensation: not just pain, but a pull that seemed to link her mind to his, a whisper of his thoughts ricocheting in her head.

Your confidence blinds you, Ravenshade. You’ll never learn control until you learn to surrender.

The voice was not Damian’s, but something deeper, resonating within the cursed magic that bound her lineage. The curse whispered a single line in both their minds, a cold echo that seemed to pulse with the rune on the stone:

“Two strands entwine; the bond awakens.”

A cold dread settled over her, but also a strange clarity. She realized, in that split second, that the wound was more than a physical injury—it was a conduit, a cursed bond forming between them.

Regaining her composure, Lyra twisted her wrist, using the momentum of the blow to spin away. She planted her foot, slipped the blade back, and with a swift, fluid motion, sliced across Damian’s forearm. The rapier sang, a thin line of blood blossoming on his skin. The moment their weapons crossed again, the same electric surge leapt, now striking both of them simultaneously.

We are bound, a faint echo rang in their shared mind, by curse and steel.

A gasp rippled through the crowd. Master Voss raised an eyebrow, his stoic mask breaking for a heartbeat.

“Worthy,” he murmured. “The curse awakens early in you, Lyra. And you, Blackthorn, have a power that seeks its counterpart.”

She’s more than a fire‑brand. There’s something… unbreakable in her spirit. If I can bend that, I’ll have more than a rival—I’ll have a weapon.

Damian steadied himself, his eyes flashing—not with anger, but with a cold, calculating interest. He lowered his rapier, the tip hovering just above Lyra’s chest.

“Seems we’re destined to dance together,” he said, voice low, a hint of amusement threaded through the danger. “Shall we continue, or shall we see what this… bond does for us?”

Lyra tightened her grip on the hilt, feeling the blood on her hand mixing with the lingering hum of the curse. She swallowed, the taste of iron sharp on her tongue, and nodded. “Let’s see.”

The duel resumed, now more than a test of skill—it became a duel of wills, of hidden histories, of a curse that had already begun to tie their fates together. The crowd watched, breaths held, as two sparks—one fiery, one icy—clashed in the courtyard of a place that promised both salvation and ruin.

Behind the stones, unseen by any eyes, a faint rune glowed brighter, as if acknowledging the new bond forged on its altar. The academy’s ancient heart beat once more, echoing the promise that every choice here would be a step toward either breaking or deepening the curse that lay at its core.

And so the Binding begins, Lyra thought, feeling the weight of destiny settle on her shoulders. If I can learn to control this… this link, perhaps I can finally free my family’s name. And if he’s as dangerous as they say, perhaps he’ll be the key—or the knife that cuts me in half.

The first strike had been made. The echo of that strike would reverberate through every hallway, every secret, and every whispered promise that the academy held. The Binding had started—by steel, by blood, by a curse that refused to stay dormant.

Chapter 2 – The Whispering Hall

The morning after the duel still clung to the academy like a thin veil of fog. Lyra awoke in the dormitory of House Ember, the wooden beams groaning softly as the building settled. She lay on a narrow cot, a thin quilt pulled over her shoulders, listening to the distant clatter of steel from the training yard.

A soft knock on the door announced the entrance of a girl with bright amber hair, tied in a practical braid.

“Morning, Lyra. You survived the first test.”

Eira Frost—Lyra’s roommate—slid the door open, a grin tugging at the corners of her mouth. “Do you want coffee? Or do you prefer the bitter taste of blood still on your tongue?”

Lyra managed a weak smile. “Coffee, please.”

The two girls walked down the narrow hallway, their footsteps echoing off the stone. The academy’s corridors were a labyrinth of arches, each lined with banners bearing the sigils of the various houses. As they turned a corner, a low, almost inaudible susurrus filled the air.

It was the Whispering Hall, a narrow passage that run beneath the main library, its walls covered in centuries‑old runes that glowed faintly when touched by light. The whispers were not mere drafts; they were the echo of spells spoken long ago—fragments of curses, promises, and lost histories that lingered like smoke.

Lyra paused, feeling the same electric tingling she’d felt in the duel’s wound. The curse that bound her seemed to tighten around her wrist, pulling her toward the far wall where a rune pulsed a dull amber.

“Seek the source, and you may find the key.”

She could not tell if the words came from the hall or from the curse itself.

“Did you hear that?” Eira whispered, eyes wide. “It’s… it’s the hall speaking.”

Lyra nodded, the scar on her cheek throbbing. “It feels like it’s pulling at something inside me.”

The two girls stepped closer to the wall. As they touched the rune, a faint, cold breeze brushed their faces and the stone seemed to shift under their fingertips, revealing a narrow slit in the floor. A staircase spiraled down into darkness.

“Should we…?” Eira began, but Lyra lifted a hand, the resolve in her eyes unmistakable.

“Whatever this is, it’s part of the curse. If I can understand it, maybe I can find a way to break it.”

Together they descended the stone steps, their torches casting long, wavering shadows on the walls. The air grew colder, and the whispering grew louder, now a chorus of half‑formed sentences in an ancient tongue. Lyra strained to catch bits of meaning:

*“…blood bound… *…the night fire… *…the fallen… *…return…

It was a tangled tapestry of loss and promise.

At the bottom of the stairs, they entered a vaulted chamber. In its center stood a pedestal of black marble, atop which rested a small, cracked crystal the size of a fist. The crystal pulsed with a deep violet light, matching the sky they’d seen from the courtyard.

Lyra stepped forward, the curse’s tug intensifying. As her fingertips brushed the crystal, a surge of memories flooded her—flames licking at the edges of a dark forest, a voice chanting in a language she could not understand, a feeling of being bound to something far older than the academy itself.

The crystal shattered with a sound like breaking ice, releasing a cascade of silver motes that rose like fireflies and vanished into the vaulted ceiling. In the silence that followed, a single voice, thin and resonant, echoed through the hall:

“The bond is forged, but the key lies elsewhere. Seek the Ember of the First Flame, and the curse will yield.”

Lyra stumbled back, breath ragged. The curse’s grip released for a heartbeat, then tightened again, as if testing her resolve.

Eira placed a steady hand on Lyra’s shoulder. “We need to tell Master Voss. This… this changes everything.”

Lyra hesitated. The curse had revealed a clue, but the words were cryptic. She remembered the ancient fire that had taken her village, the ember that had never gone out in her dreams. Could that be the “First Flame” the whisper referenced?

“Not yet,” Lyra said, voice trembling. “If we rush, the curse will tighten. I need to think.”

Eira nodded, understanding the weight of a decision made too quickly. “Then we’ll stay a while. I’ll keep watch at the entrance. If anyone comes…”

Lyra stared at the shattered crystal, its fragments scattered across the stone floor, each glinting in the torchlight. The whispers in the hall faded, as if satisfied that someone had listened.

She tucked a small piece of the crystal into the pocket of her tunic, feeling the faint pulse against her skin. It was a reminder—a map, a promise, a warning.

The two girls climbed back up the stairs, the stone walls now eerily quiet. When they emerged into the corridor, the academy seemed unchanged, but Lyra knew that an invisible line had been drawn between her and the ancient magic that rose from the earth beneath her feet.

As they walked toward the Great Hall for breakfast, Lyra caught sight of Damian across the courtyard, his eyes still fixed on her, as if measuring the distance between their worlds.

“He knows,” she thought, the curse whispering faintly, “and he will be part of whatever this quest becomes.”

The day stretched ahead, filled with lessons, training, and the simmering curiosity that now pulsed in her veins. The Whispering Hall had given her a clue, but the path it illuminated was shrouded in shadow.

Lyra resolved to find the Ember of the First Flame, whatever it meant, before the curse could define her future.

The hunt had begun.

Chapter 3 – The Nightmare Trial

The sun had risen high over the academy when the morning bell tolled, its deep, resonant tone echoing through the cloisters and striking a nervous rhythm in Lyra’s chest. The students filed into the great hall for the day’s first official trial – the Nightmare Trial – a rite of passage designed to test a novice’s resistance to the dark magics that seeped through the stone of the academy.

Master Voss stood on a raised dais, his silvered beard catching the morning light. He lifted a hand, and the chatter fell to a hush.

“Welcome, fledglings,” he intoned. “Today you will confront the shadows that dwell within you. The trial is not a simple combat; it is a test of mind, heart, and the very curse that binds you.”

Lyra felt the familiar tug of the curse at the base of her neck. Damian, already positioned opposite her, gave her a curt nod – a silent acknowledgment that their fates were now intertwined.

The arena was a circular stone chamber, its walls lined with black basalt that seemed to drink the light. In the centre lay a shallow pool of black water, still and mirror‑like. Around its rim, ancient sigils glowed faintly, pulsing in rhythm with the low hum that filled the air.

Voss raised a staff, and the sigils flared brighter. The water’s surface rippled, and a mist rose, coiling like a serpent.

“Enter the darkness, and let the nightmare claim you.”

Lyra stepped forward, her heart a drumbeat. As she crossed the threshold, the world tilted. The stone walls melted away, replaced by an endless forest of twisted black trees, their branches clawing at a sky of perpetual twilight.

She was alone – or so it seemed – until a figure emerged from the shadows: a younger version of herself, hair singed, eyes hollow, clutching a dying ember.

“You think you can escape the fire?” the phantom hissed. “You are the ash of what once burned.”

The nightmare was a mirror of her deepest fear: that the curse would consume her, that she would become nothing but smoke. The phantom lunged, the ember in its hand flaring to a sickly orange, casting grotesque shadows.

Lyra’s instinct was to swing her blade, but the ground beneath her was soft earth, not stone. She felt the weight of the curse surge, a cold electric current threading through her veins.

“Remember the first flame,” a whisper echoed in her mind – the voice of the curse itself, now sounding almost protective. “From ember to blaze, you are the spark.”

She closed her eyes, focusing on the memory of the night her village burned. The heat had been unbearable, but she remembered one thing: a single ember that refused to die, cradled in her palm as the world fell around her.

Opening her eyes, Lyra lifted her hand, and a faint blue flame ignited on the palm of her other hand – a manifestation of the curse’s power, now under her control. The flame flickered, growing steadier with each breath.

She turned to the phantom, the ember in its hand winking out. “You are not my end,” she said, voice strong. “I am the ember that will never be fully snuffed.”

The phantom shrieked, its form dissolving into a cloud of ash that swirled around Lyra’s feet. As the ash settled, the forest faded, and the stone chamber reappeared. The black water in the center rippled, reflecting her face – not just Lyra, but a version tempered by fire.

Damian stood across from her, his own trial completed in a flash of icy shards that shattered into crystalline snowflakes. He gave her a terse, “Well done,” before the bell rang, signaling the end of the trial.

Master Voss lowered his staff, his eyes lingering on the two newly bound novices.

“You have survived the first nightmare,” he said. “But the true darkness lies deeper within these walls. Keep your eyes open – the curse will test you again.”

Lyra felt the curse’s pull recede, but the sensation of a lingering whisper remained, promising more to come. She glanced at Damian; their eyes met, and for a moment, the bond between them pulsed like a heartbeat.

“We have only begun,” she thought, the curse humming faintly, “and the ember is still warm.”

The trial was over, but the hunt for the First Flame had only just begun.

Chapter 4 – Forbidden Library

The bell that announced the end of the Morning Lessons rang like a warning shot, and the courtyard emptied as students fled to their respective houses. Lyra lingered, her thoughts still tangled in the whispers of the Hall and the ember‑flame she had conjured. She felt the pull of the curse like a low tide, urging her toward something hidden.

“Lyra?” a voice called behind her. She turned to see Eira, clutching a steaming mug of tea, her amber hair tangled from the night’s sleep.

“Did you see the… thing in the hall?” Lyra asked, keeping her voice low.

Eira shook her head, eyebrows knitting. “Just the usual rumors. But there’s a place the older students keep quiet about – the Forbidden Library. It’s said to house the original tomes of the Cursed Arts, the ones the founders sealed away when the covenant was first made.

If we could get inside… maybe we could find something about this curse.”

Lyra’s pulse quickened. The forbidden wing had always been a legend, a place whispered about in the dorm rooms, a curiosity for reckless youths. The idea of accessing it felt both exhilarating and dangerous.

“Do you know how?”

“You’ll need a key,” Eira replied, lowering her voice further. “Someone told me that Professor Voss once carried a silver key on a chain at his belt. He only uses it for the oldest sections of the library – the Hall of Sealed Scrolls. If we can… borrow it, we might slip past the wards.

Lyra weighed the risk. The curse was already tugging at her, but something in her gut – perhaps that whisper of the ember – told her she had to try.

“Alright,” she said. “We’ll watch Voss after the next lesson. He always returns to his study at dusk.”

The afternoon dragged on. Lyra attended a lesson on “Cursed Glyphs and Their Counter‑chants,” a lecture that felt more like a warning. Master Voss moved about the room, chalk dust clinging to his sleeves, his eyes often flicking toward the far wall, where a narrow doorway led to a corridor of stacked bookshelves.

When the bell finally rang, Voss gathered his notes, slipping the silver key from a hidden pouch and tucking it into the inner lining of his robe. He turned, and with a curt nod to the class, exited the hall.

Lyra and Eira slipped through the doorway just as Voss passed. The corridor was dim, the air heavy with the scent of old paper and lingering incense.

“Make sure you’re swift,” whispered Eira, already moving toward a side arch leading deeper into the library’s bowels.

Lyra followed, heart pounding. The key was hidden under a loose stone near a statue of an ancient sorceress. With a careful tug, Lyra lifted the stone, revealing a shallow cavity where the key lay waiting.

She slipped the key into her palm, feeling a faint chill as metal met skin. The curse seemed to hum, as if acknowledging her boldness.

They moved through the labyrinthine stacks, the only sound their boots on the polished stone floor. The further they went, the more the air thrummed with latent magic. Shelves rose into darkness, each laden with leather‑bound volumes that seemed to pulse with concealed power.

At the center of the Forbidden Library stood a massive oak door, etched with a sigil of a phoenix in flame, its feathers interwoven with chains. A rune in the center glowed a sickly green – a ward to keep unworthy hands at bay.

Lyra raised the silver key and pressed it into the lock. The lock clicked, and the door shuddered open, revealing a vaulted chamber lit by a single shaft of light that fell from a set of cracked stained glass high above.

Inside, rows upon rows of ancient scrolls lay on stone tables, each unrolled to reveal inked diagrams of curses, binding rites, and the first covenant that bound the academy to the land. In the very center, on a pedestal of black marble, rested a thick, vellum‑bound tome titled “The Covenant of the First Flame.”

Lyra’s breath caught. The title reverberated with the whisper she had heard in the Whispering Hall and the ember fragment within her.

She reached for the tome, but the moment her fingers brushed the cover, a soft voice echoed through the chamber:

“Only the blood of the true ember may read the covenant.”

Lyra stared at her own palm, where a faint blue flame still flickered, a gift from the curse.

“Damian?” she whispered, half‑expecting his presence.

The room remained still, but the flame on her hand grew brighter, casting a warm glow over the pages.

Instinctively, Lyra opened the book. The vellum was brittle, yet the text glowed with a faint amber light, as if the words themselves were alive. The first passage read:

"When the ember endures the night, and the blood of the cursed bearer bears the mark of the ancient fire, the first flame shall be revealed. Only then can the binding be broken, or the curse deepened."

She felt the curse’s whisper again, softer this time, almost like a sigh:

"Seek the ember, Lyra. The key lies within you, but the world must know of it."

Lyra’s mind raced. The covenant spoke of a physical ember, a relic perhaps hidden somewhere in the academy’s foundations, or a symbolic ember within herself.

Eira, eyes wide, whispered, “We can’t stay here. If Voss returns…

Lyra snapped the book shut, gently placing it back on the pedestal. The door behind them began to creak, the ward reacting to their departure.

They fled the chamber, sealing the oak door behind them. The silver key slipped from Lyra’s grasp, clattering onto the stone floor. She retrieved it, tucking it into her tunic.

Back in the corridor, they stopped to catch their breath. The fluorescent glow of the ward faded, and the library seemed to settle back into its ancient slumber.

“Whatever that was,” Eira panted, “we’ve got something huge. The First Flame… it’s real.”

Lyra nodded, the ember in her hand still smoldering faintly.

"You have taken the first step," the curse whispered, "but the path is fraught with shadows. Keep the ember close. Trust no one – except perhaps the one who already walks with you in darkness."

She glanced across the hallway where Damian stood, his eyes narrowed, as if he too sensed the shift in power.

Lyra realized that the hunt for the First Flame would not be a solitary quest. The cursed bond between them pulsed like a compass, pointing toward a destiny neither could yet fully comprehend.

The night fell over the academy, but within the walls of the Forbidden Library, the ember’s secret burned brighter than any torch.

The search had truly begun.

Chapter 5 – Rivalry Ignites

The morning after the Forbidden Library escapade, the courtyard buzzed with whispered gossip. Students clustered in tight circles, eyes darting toward Lyra and Damian as they passed. Even the stone statues seemed to lean in, as if the academy itself were listening.

Lyra tried to focus on her morning spellwork. Master Voss had assigned the class a simple transmutation exercise: turn a wilted herb into a fresh sprig of mint. It was a test of precision, patience, and the ability to channel personal energy without letting the curse flare.

She knelt on the cold flagstones, cupped a handful of dead rosemary, and whispered the incantation taught the day before. The words felt familiar, but a faint tremor of the curse tugged at the edge of her mind, threatening to spill over.

“Steady,” she murmured, feeling the ember flare in her palm. A thin blue flame licked the rosemary, coaxing it back to life. Within seconds, the rosemary unfurled into a vivid sprig of mint, its scent filling the air.

A ripple of applause rose from the surrounding students. Lyra smiled, the brief triumph quieting the knot in her chest—until a silvery chuckle cut through the courtyard.

“Impressive, Ravenshade.” Damian stepped into the circle, his violet eyes glittering. He raised a hand, and the mint sprig wilted instantly, turning to ash.

The crowd gasped. Lyra’s heartbeat hammered against her ribs. “What did you—?” she began, but Damian’s smile was already gone, replaced by a colder, more calculating stare.

“Don’t think this was a simple trick,” he said, his voice low enough that only she could hear. “I used a fragment of the curse itself to reverse your spell. You may have the ember, but I have learned to bend it—just enough to counter you.

“Is that… a threat?” Lyra asked, trying to keep her tone even.

“Consider it a challenge.” Damian replied, turning and walking away, his coat swirling. “The Academy’s first trial is over. Now the real competition begins.”

The words lingered in the air like a cold draft. Lyra felt the curse’s pulse quicken, as if it were listening for a duel.

Later that afternoon, Master Voss announced an impromptu duel tournament called the Rivalry Ignites. The purpose was to sharpen the novices’ combat abilities and to see how well they could control the cursed arts under pressure.

“Pairs will be drawn at random,” Voss declared, his voice echoing off the high vaulted ceiling of the training yard. “Each duel will consist of three rounds: a test of physical combat, a test of magical manipulation, and a final test of strategic thinking. The winner of each round gains a fragment of the Cursed Seal, an ancient rune that, when collected, can be used to repair or break curses.”

A hush fell over the students. The mention of the Cursed Seal sent a ripple of anticipation through the crowd—rumors spoke of the Seal as a relic that could amplify or nullify any bond.

Lyra’s name was called for the first round. She was paired against a lanky boy named Joren, a prodigy from House Slate known for his agility and quicksilver reflexes. The first round was a physical combat test. The bell rang, and the two novices lunged at each other.

Joren darted forward, delivering a swift kick to Lyra’s thigh. She gritted her teeth, feeling the curse’s lingering sting in her muscles. Instinctively, she raised her blade, parrying a follow‑up strike and counter‑attacking with a precise thrust that grazed Joren’s side, leaving a shallow cut.

The crowd roared. The bell rang again, ending the round. The judges awarded the point to Lyra for her composure under pressure.

The second round was magical manipulation. Both novices were given a crystal orb filled with a volatile black mist. Their task: cleanse the orb using only their own magical energy, without breaking the containment.

Lyra placed her hand on the orb, feeling the familiar electric hum of the curse surge through her. She closed her eyes and visualized the ember within her, recalling the blue flame that had manifested in the Whispering Hall. The mist within the orb began to swirl, coalescing into a tighter, darker vortex.

She whispered an incantation of binding, and a thin line of bright amber light shot from her palm, sealing the vortex and solidifying the orb into a clear sphere. The crowd gasped; it was a move she had never attempted before.

Joren, on the other hand, relied on a rapid flick of his wrist, releasing a burst of cold air that froze the mist in place. The judges whispered among themselves, then awarded the round to Lyra again, citing her creative use of the curse.

The final round was strategic thinking. A board was set up with three pieces: a miniature academy tower, a miniature figure of the Cursed Seal, and a figure representing a hidden artifact—the Ember of the First Flame. Each contestant could move one piece per turn, trying to either protect the Ember or capture the Seal.

Damian entered the arena for the third round, his presence causing a ripple of tension. He took his seat opposite Lyra, his violet eyes never leaving hers.

The first move was Damian’s. He slid the tower piece toward the Ember, shielding it with a line of imagined magical wards. Lyra responded by moving the Seal piece closer to the tower, attempting to force Damian to break his defensive line.
